# Assignment 8 — Identifying Risks in a Medical Case Study

**Course:** ITAI 4375 — AI in Healthcare  
**Date:** March 17, 2026  

---

## Problem

Healthcare AI is most valuable when it addresses real clinical complexity. This assignment analyzed a published medical case study of a 70-year-old male with poorly controlled type 2 diabetes, hypertension, diabetic foot ulceration, and acute depression — a patient whose overlapping conditions create a compounding web of risks. The task was to identify three major health risks and evaluate how AI tools could help manage each one.

## Approach

I performed a structured risk analysis, identifying the three most clinically significant risks from the case details:

1. **Uncontrolled hyperglycemia progressing to diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)** — the acute crisis driving hospitalization
2. **Diabetic foot ulceration with high infection and amputation potential** — a serious complication requiring continuous monitoring
3. **Polypharmacy-driven medication non-adherence** — a behavioral and systemic factor contributing to rapid readmission

For each risk, I explained its clinical significance, the consequences of leaving it unaddressed, and how specific AI technologies could help manage or reduce the risk.

## Results & Key AI Applications

- **Risk 1 (DKA):** Predictive glucose monitoring algorithms and closed-loop insulin delivery systems can detect dangerous glucose trends before they escalate
- **Risk 2 (Diabetic Foot):** Computer vision wound assessment tools can track ulcer progression from smartphone photos, enabling remote monitoring without requiring clinic visits
- **Risk 3 (Medication Non-Adherence):** AI-enabled medication adherence platforms using smart pill dispensers and behavioral nudges can identify non-adherence patterns early and trigger outreach

## What I Learned

This was one of the most practically grounding assignments of the course. It forced me to think about AI not as an abstract capability but as a set of tools applied to a specific, messy, real human situation. I also learned that risk identification itself is a clinical skill — naming a risk precisely is the first step toward addressing it.
