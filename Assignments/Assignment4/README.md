## Problem

Many of the deadliest cancers — pancreatic, ovarian, esophageal — lack effective early screening methods and are typically diagnosed at advanced stages when treatment options are severely limited. This assignment examined the Galleri multi-cancer early detection (MCED) test, developed by GRAIL Inc., which promises to detect over 50 cancer types from a single blood draw using AI.

## Approach

I conducted a comprehensive analysis of the Galleri test covering four areas:

- **Biological mechanism:** How cell-free DNA (cfDNA) methylation patterns serve as cancer biomarkers
- **AI integration:** How the machine learning classifier (trained on 100,000+ blood samples) detects cancer signals and predicts tissue of origin
- **Clinical validation:** Review of published sensitivity and specificity data across cancer types
- **Ethical and access considerations:** Cost barriers, false positive implications, and equity in deployment

## Results & Key Findings

The Galleri test represents a genuine paradigm shift in oncology screening. Its AI classifier performs two critical functions simultaneously — detecting the presence of a cancer signal and predicting where in the body the cancer originated, which guides follow-up diagnostics efficiently. However, sensitivity varies significantly by cancer stage: the test performs far better at detecting late-stage cancers than early-stage ones, which is precisely where early detection would have the most impact.

## What I Learned

This assignment taught me to evaluate an AI medical tool not just on its technical promise but on its real-world clinical utility. A test that sounds revolutionary in a press release may have significant limitations in practice. I also developed a deeper appreciation for the ethical stakes of AI diagnostics — a false positive result doesn't just affect a lab report; it triggers anxiety, invasive follow-up procedures, and financial costs for real patients.
