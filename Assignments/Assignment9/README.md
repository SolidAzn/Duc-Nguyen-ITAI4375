# Assignment 9 — Critiquing a Natural Language Processing Study in Healthcare

**Course:** ITAI 4375 — AI in Healthcare  
**Date:** March 23, 2026  

---

## Problem

Up to 80% of healthcare documentation exists as unstructured free text — progress notes, discharge summaries, operative reports — that traditional computational tools cannot readily analyze. Natural Language Processing (NLP) has emerged as a promising solution. This assignment critically evaluated a specific NLP application described by ForeSee Medical, which uses NLP to extract clinically relevant information from unstructured electronic health records (EHRs).

## Approach

I structured the critique around four questions:
1. What is NLP and how does it function in this healthcare context?
2. What are the two most significant benefits of this application?
3. What are the two most significant limitations?
4. On balance, do the benefits outweigh the risks?

My analysis drew on recent peer-reviewed literature from high-impact journals to ground each claim in evidence.

## Results & Key Findings

**Benefits identified:**
- Accelerated clinical documentation review, reducing the time clinicians spend manually searching through records
- Improved diagnostic coding accuracy, reducing billing errors and improving downstream data quality for analytics and research

**Limitations identified:**
- Patient data privacy risks, particularly around re-identification and data breach exposure
- Potential for algorithmic errors in clinical contexts where a misread note could influence a treatment decision

**Overall verdict:** NLP offers substantial value to healthcare organizations — but only when deployed with appropriate governance, transparency, and human oversight. The benefits are real; so are the risks.

## What I Learned

Critical thinking about AI tools is a skill, and this assignment was an exercise in developing it. I learned to distinguish between marketing claims and peer-reviewed evidence, between theoretical capabilities and real-world performance. I also became more attuned to how the framing of a study can obscure as much as it reveals — a single-vendor case study is a different kind of evidence than a multi-site randomized evaluation.
